Section 01

The Four Generations of Computers

Click any era card to expand details. Each generation was defined by its switching technology — the thing that made logic possible.

📋
2018 Exam Alert! Question 2 in your past paper asked to "categorise each invention according to its era in the history of computing: pre-mechanical, mechanical or electronic." This timeline directly prepares you for that!
1945 – 1955
1st Generation — Vacuum Tubes
ENIAC IAS UNIVAC
▼ tap to expand
Technology: Vacuum tubes (like giant light bulbs that switch on/off to represent 1s and 0s).

Size: Room-sized machines. ENIAC weighed 30 tonnes and contained 18,000 vacuum tubes.

Problems: Generated enormous heat, consumed huge amounts of power, tubes burned out constantly — like changing lightbulbs every few minutes.

Key Machine: IAS (von Neumann's machine at Princeton) — introduced the stored-program concept which is still how computers work today!
1955 – 1965
2nd Generation — Transistors
IBM 7094 DEC PDP-1
▼ tap to expand
Technology: Transistors — invented at Bell Labs 1947. Same function as vacuum tubes but tiny, reliable, low power, cheap.

Size: Shrank from room-sized to cabinet-sized.

Key improvement: Reliability shot up dramatically. No more constant tube replacements.

Analogy: Replacing a bonfire with a candle — same light, fraction of the energy and mess.
1965 – 1980
3rd Generation — Integrated Circuits
IBM 360 DEC PDP-8
▼ tap to expand
Technology: Integrated Circuits (ICs) — multiple transistors on a single chip. Invented ~1958 by Jack Kilby.

Moore's Law Born: Gordon Moore observed in 1965 that the number of transistors on a chip doubles roughly every 2 years. This prediction held for decades!

Key innovation: IBM System/360 — first family of computers with compatible software across models. Businesses could upgrade without rewriting everything.
1980 – Present
4th Generation — VLSI / Microprocessors
Intel 8080 Pentium ARM
▼ tap to expand
Technology: Very Large Scale Integration (VLSI) — millions then billions of transistors on one chip.

The microprocessor: The entire CPU on a single chip. Intel 4004 (1971) had 2,300 transistors. Modern chips have 50+ billion.

Impact: Personal computers, laptops, smartphones — computing became accessible to everyone, not just governments and corporations.

The IAS Machine — Why It Matters

The IAS (Princeton Institute for Advanced Study) machine designed by John von Neumann in 1946 introduced the architecture that almost every computer since has followed. Knowing this is knowing your roots.

🔑 The von Neumann Concept: Before this, programs were "wired in" to the machine. Von Neumann said — what if we store the program in memory, just like data? Then we can change what the computer does just by loading a different program. This single idea is why you can run WhatsApp, Chrome, and a game on the same device.
Section 04

Computer Performance — The Equation

This section is heavily tested in exams — look at Section B Question 1 in your 2018 paper. You need to know this cold.

📋
2018 Exam Q1: Directly asks you to calculate average CPI and execution time using this exact formula. Master this and that's 20 marks in your pocket.
THE PERFORMANCE EQUATION
T = Ic × CPI × τ
T = Execution Time (seconds)
Ic = Instruction Count (number of instructions)
CPI = Cycles Per Instruction (average)
τ (tau) = Clock Cycle Time = 1 / Clock Rate (Hz)
⏱️

Clock Rate (Hz)

How many cycles per second. 3.4 GHz = 3.4 billion cycles per second. Faster clock = shorter cycle time τ.

🔄

CPI

Different instructions take different cycles. Simple ADD might be 1 cycle, a divide could be 20+. Average CPI = weighted average across all instruction types.

📊

Average CPI Formula

CPIavg = Σ (CPIi × fi) where fi is the fraction of instructions of type i.

🏭 Factory Analogy: Imagine a factory producing cars. T = how long total. Ic = number of tasks to do. CPI = how many machine cycles each task needs. τ = how fast the machines run. Want faster production? Speed up machines (↑ clock rate), reduce steps per task (↓ CPI), or reduce total tasks (↓ Ic).

🔥 Challenge Quiz

Q1. The von Neumann architecture's most revolutionary idea was:

✅ Stored-program concept! Before von Neumann, you'd rewire the hardware to change what the computer did. He said — put the program IN memory. One machine, infinite programs. Every computer you've ever used runs on this idea.

Q2. Moore's Law states that transistor count doubles roughly every:

✅ ~2 years! Gordon Moore made this observation in 1965. It's held remarkably well, though the pace has been slowing as we approach physical limits of silicon.

Q3. A program has 1,000,000 instructions and runs on a 2 GHz processor with average CPI of 2. What's the execution time?

✅ 0.001 seconds! T = Ic × CPI × τ = 1,000,000 × 2 × (1/2,000,000,000) = 2,000,000 / 2,000,000,000 = 0.001s. Get comfortable with this calculation — the exam WILL test you on it.

Q4. The Abacus belongs to which era in computing history?

✅ Pre-mechanical! The abacus uses beads to represent numbers — no gears, no electricity, just physical manipulation. This directly answers Q2 in your 2018 exam paper! Pre-mechanical = manual tools (abacus, Napier's bones, finger-counting). Mechanical = gears (Pascaline, Schickard's calculator). Electronic = electricity.
